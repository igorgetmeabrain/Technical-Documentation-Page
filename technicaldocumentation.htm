How to Solve a Rubik's Cube

When the Hungarian architect Ernő Rubik invented his eponymous Cube in 1974, little did he know that it would become the world’s best-selling toy, with over 350 million cubes sold worldwide to date.

Still, it is estimated that only around 1 in 20 people who own a cube know how to solve it.

The Beginner Method is a simple approach to solving, which can be mastered by anyone, with just a little patience and perseverance.

If you have never solved a cube, this guide could take you from zero to hero in under an hour!

FUN FACTS

The Rubik’s Cube has 26 pieces (cubies):
6 centres, 12 edges and 8 corners.

The centres are fixed to a central core, allowing the other 20 cubies to move freely around them.

Notation

Rubik's Cube Notation was first developed by self-confessed metragrobologist David Singmaster in 1979, as a simple means of indicating rotations of the different faces of the cube.

Learning this notation will help you to follow the simple algorithms (sequences of moves) which are used during each phase of the solve.

This notation indicates a clockwise quarter-turn (90°) of the corresponding face of the cube:
R = RIGHT F = FRONT U = UP
L = LEFT B = BACK D = DOWN NOTE: Clockwise rotation is defined as if the relevant face were facing you (e.g. L rotates clockwise towards you whilst R rotates away from you). To denote an anti-clockwise quarter-turn, the face letter is followed by a prime symbol (').
So, whilst R indicates a quarter-turn away from you, R' (AKA "R Prime") is a quarter-turn towards you. Finally, you can indicate a half-turn of a face by adding a 2 after the letter (e.g. R2).
NOTE: Half-turns can be made in either direction.

This notation is independent of the colours of the faces. F is always RED throughout this guide, but in practice F is whichever is facing you as you execute any given algorithm.

Bottom Cross

The Beginner Method is a layer-by-layer approach to solving the cube.

Conventionally, we solve with the white face on the bottom, so let's first orient the cube (turn the whole cube) so that the white centre is facing down.

We will begin by solving the white edge pieces, forming a white cross on the bottom of the cube.

This is an intuitive part of the solve, so see if you can work it out for yourself.

A simple approach is first to turn all of the white edges, so that they face upwards (around the yellow centre on the top face)... ...then line one up (with U moves) to match a middle centre. Half-turn the white edge to the bottom layer.
Repeat this process until all white edges are solved.

The six centres are the only pieces which always remain fixed, relative to each other.
So, it can be helpful to think of solving the cube around these fixed centres.

FUN FACTS

There are more than 43 quintillion (or 43 billion billion) possible scrambled positions (or permutations) of a cube.

However, some very clever people eventually proved (in 2010) that ANY scrambled cube can theoretically be solved in 20 moves, or fewer.

This is known as God's Number, because this is usually far beyond the capabilities of even the cleverest human brains on our planet.

Bottom Corners

  • Now it’s time to complete the bottom layer of the cube.
  • It's possible to do this intuitively, but be careful to preserve the solved white edges in the process.
  • White corner pieces fall into four cases, depending on their position and orientation.
  • First, let's look for a white corner piece in the top (yellow) layer.
  • Using the colours of the middle centres as a guide, turn the U face until the corner is directly above the position into which it needs to be solved, orienting the cube so that it is the front right corner.
If white is facing sideways, use the simple insertion moves illustrated in the CASE 1 and CASE 2 animations.
If white is facing up, follow the CASE 3 animation, which is a setup sequence which arrives at (and then follows) CASE 1 insertion. Having solved all corners which were in the top layer, any remaining unsolved ones must be in the bottom layer (CASE 4). Again, holding it in the front right position, kick the piece from the bottom into the top layer, with the sequence R U R' U'. It may now be solved with one of the CASES above.

R U R′ U′ is also known as 'Sexy Move' (yeah, really). This (like other similar sequences) is easy to execute and leaves most of the cube undisturbed, hence why it's so handy.

FUN FACTS

A notable example of a scramble which requires a full 20 moves to solve, is the Superflip.

A Superflipped cube is solved except that all middle edges are flipped the wrong way round.

The 20 move solution is:
U R2 F B R B2 R U2
L B2 R U' D' R2 F
R' L B2 U2 F2

Middle Layer

Solving this layer requires only the insertion of four edge pieces, as there are no corners here.

This is where we transition from the intuitive into the algorithmic phase of solving.

It is even possible to solve the middle layer intuitively, but one simple algorithm will do the trick.

FUN FACTS

Speedcubers compete to solve the Rubik's Cube (and other twisty puzzles) as quickly as possible.

There are many different methods, but the most popular is CFOP, which is favoured by many of the world’s top speedcubers.

Jessica Fridrich pioneered this layer-by-layer method, essentially an extremely optimised Beginner method.

Learning the full CFOP method requires memorising at least 87 algorithms!

Orient Top Cross

One layer to go…

This is where algorithms come to the fore, as it is now extremely difficult to solve pieces intuitively, whilst preserving the two solved layers.

Thankfully, one simple algorithm can orient the yellow cross.

Execute the following algorithm with the yellow face upwards:
F R U R' U' F' NOTE: We're interested only in the pattern of the yellow edges on the upper face.
Ignore the yellow corners.

Each time you execute the algorithm, you cycle through the above patterns, without disturbing the bottom or middle layer. Repeat until a cross is formed.

Permute Top Cross

Now we permute the yellow edges.

This means shuffling them until they are in their solved positions, relative to the rest of the cube.

Again, one algorithm suffices, though you may need to execute it twice, if all four edges are swapped.

Rotate the U face, until you find two adjacent edges which need to be swapped to match them with their corresponding middle layer centres. This algorithm swaps the front and left edges - in this case blue and red.

There are three possible permutations of the yellow edges:
1. Two edges are swapped 2. All four edges are swapped 3. All edges are solved

Orient Top Corners

Now you are only one step away from solving the cube!

There is a simple algorithm which orients the yellow corners, but you must follow it very carefully.

Begin by holding the cube with an incorrectly oriented corner in the front right position.
The algorithm is: R' D' R D
You will need to execute this algorithm either twice or four times, to orient the corner correctly. Having fixed one corner, now turn the U face ONLY to bring another corner to the front right position.
Repeat the process until all corners are solved.
NOTE: The cube may seem pretty scrambled as you execute this final step, but do not fear!
If you have done it correctly,
YOU HAVE SOLVED THE CUBE!

Oops! Did it all go wrong? Never mind, go back and try again!
Remember, NEVER turn the whole cube whilst solving this final stage, only the U face.
